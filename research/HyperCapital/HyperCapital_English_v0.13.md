## Abstract

This research aims to address the structural challenges posed by Platform Capitalism, particularly its systematic extraction and alienation of users' social capital. The existing model, represented by tech giants like Google and Meta, monetizes users' social capital (information, data, influence, reputation) through centralized networks and opaque algorithms, leading to severe value distribution inequities and innovation suppression. To resolve this core contradiction, this study employs a Design Science Research methodology to construct and evaluate an innovative digital asset named **HyperCapital**. HyperCapital is defined as a programmable, standardized digital asset based on decentralized consensus protocols, essentially serving as a digital credit certificate that encapsulates and authenticates individuals' social capital contributions in digital networks. Its ultimate goal is to serve an "Economics of Well-being," shifting economic activities from profit maximization toward enhancing the overall well-being of participant communities. This paper further elaborates on the pathway to realize HyperCapital through deep synthesis of "new technology" (blockchain-based consensus infrastructure) and "new economic models" (collaborative innovation and consensus economy). Finally, this research demonstrates the feasibility of HyperCapital in building small-scale, value-internalized well-being economic models through theoretical framework design of the **Mycelium Protocol** and simulated case validation of **Zu.Coffee**. The core contribution of this thesis lies in providing an alternative solution that combines theoretical innovation with practical pathways to transcend the existing platform economy paradigm, advancing social capital from a sociological analytical concept to an operational, programmable economic concept.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Research Background: The Twilight of Platform Capitalism

The rapid development of the digital economy has given rise to the platform capitalism model represented by Google, Meta, Amazon, and others (Srnicek, 2017). These platforms leverage their powerful network effects and technological barriers to build centralized digital ecosystems. While providing convenient services, they have also established a systematic value extraction mechanism. The core of this mechanism lies in converting users' every click, every social interaction, every data footprint—broadly defined as **Social Capital**—into the platforms' own economic profits. However, users, as the source of value creation, have not received fair compensation. According to statistics, Alphabet (Google's parent company) generated $237.86 billion in advertising revenue in 2023, accounting for 77.8% of total revenue, essentially monetizing users' search intentions and attention.

The internal contradictions of this model are becoming increasingly apparent: structural inequity in value distribution, continuous erosion of data privacy (Zuboff, 2019), information silos under algorithmic black boxes, and suppression of broader social innovation due to their quasi-monopolistic positions. The growth of existing paradigms has reached its ethical and economic limits, and the development of the digital economy is facing a critical crossroads.

### 1.2 Core Problem Statement

The core problem of this research is: **How can we design a new digital economic paradigm to replace the current extractive platform capitalism model, achieving fair authentication, reasonable pricing, and just distribution of individual social capital, thereby constructing a more inclusive and innovative "well-being economy"?**

### 1.3 Research Objectives and Contributions

To address the above question, this research is committed to:
1. **Theoretical Innovation**: Proposing and systematically elaborating "HyperCapital" as a new theory, serving as the evolutionary form of social capital in the digital age.
2. **Pathway Exploration**: Exploring the combination of blockchain technology and collaborative economic models to provide dual support for HyperCapital's technical implementation and economic sustainability.
3. **Practical Validation**: Conducting preliminary proof-of-concept through the Mycelium Protocol and Zu.Coffee simulation case.

The main contribution of this research lies in not only critiquing platform capitalism but also proposing a constructive, operational alternative solution, providing new theoretical perspectives and practical references for future digital economic governance and social organizational forms in the "post-platform era."

### 1.4 Thesis Structure

This thesis consists of seven chapters. Chapter 1 is the introduction. Chapter 2 conducts a literature review. Chapter 3 constructs the theoretical framework of HyperCapital. Chapter 4 elaborates on research design and methodology. Chapter 5 provides in-depth case analysis of the Mycelium Protocol and Zu.Coffee experiment. Chapter 6 presents the discussion. Chapter 7 offers conclusions and future prospects.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Platform Capitalism and Surveillance Capitalism Critique

This section reviews and critiques research on platform capitalism. Srnicek (2017) points out that the core characteristic of platforms is gaining competitive advantage through data extraction and network effects. Zuboff (2019) proposes the concept of "surveillance capitalism," insightfully pointing out that tech giants not only extract data but also analyze user behavioral data to predict and change user behavior, thereby achieving profit maximization. Additionally, this section introduces "Digital Labor" literature, exploring how users' unpaid labor on platforms constitutes the foundation of platform value (Fuchs, 2014).

### 2.2 Social Capital Theory and Its Limitations in the Digital Age

Social capital theory, from Bourdieu (1986), Coleman (1988) to Putnam (2000), provides a solid theoretical foundation for understanding intangible value in social networks. Bourdieu emphasizes the class attributes of social capital, Coleman focuses on its functionality, while Putnam worries about the loss of social capital in modern society. However, these classical theories were born in pre-digital or early digital eras. In the current highly interconnected digital environment, traditional social capital theory reveals its limitations in explaining the "large-scale, computable, instantaneous" new characteristics. It lacks theoretical tools for **technical authentication** and **value quantification** of social capital.

### 2.3 Decentralized Technology and Collaborative Economy

This section reviews the potential of blockchain and distributed ledger technology (DLT) as "trust machines" (The Economist, 2015). We explore research progress in building decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs), tokenomics, and collaborative networks (Voshmgir, 2019). Meanwhile, we also critique the limitations of existing attempts (such as Gitcoin, DAOstack), such as over-emphasis on technology while neglecting the endogenous sustainability of economic models, or failure to comprehensively authenticate diversified social capital. This provides background for the uniqueness and necessity of this research.

## 3. Theoretical Framework

### 3.1 Definition and Core Attributes of HyperCapital

This chapter is the theoretical core of the thesis. **HyperCapital** is formally defined as: **A programmable, standardized digital asset based on decentralized consensus protocols, essentially serving as a digital credit certificate that encapsulates and authenticates individuals' social capital contributions (information, data, influence, reputation, credit, etc.) in digital networks.**

Its core difference from traditional social capital lies in HyperCapital's aim to transform social capital that is originally embedded in specific social relationships and difficult to separate and circulate into a universally applicable, independently circulating value carrier through technological means.

Its core attributes include:
1. **Verifiability** - Technical verification of contribution authenticity
2. **Quantifiability** - Mathematical models for value quantification
3. **Programmability** - Smart contract automation
4. **Transferability** - Cross-platform value circulation

### 3.2 HyperCapital Measurement Model

To transform HyperCapital from an abstract concept into an operational, measurable construct, we propose a comprehensive evaluation model. This model breaks down HyperCapital into five core components, calculated through a weighted algorithm.

`HyperCapital Score = w₁×NPC + w₂×TRC + w₃×AIC + w₄×DCC + w₅×CCC`

Where `w` represents the weight of each component, which can be dynamically adjusted through community governance (DAO voting) to adapt to the strategic focus of collaborative networks at different development stages.

1. **Network Position Capital (NPC)**: Structural positional value of individuals in social networks, measurable through PageRank or centrality indicators.
2. **Trust Reputation Capital (TRC)**: Trust metrics accumulated based on historical behavior, calculable through weighted scoring (such as peer evaluation, successful collaboration count).
3. **Attention Influence Capital (AIC)**: Ability to obtain and distribute attention, quantifiable through content dissemination range and interaction depth.
4. **Data Contribution Capital (DCC)**: Personal data's contribution to network value creation, valued according to data quality, uniqueness, and usage frequency.
5. **Collaboration Contribution Capital (CCC)**: Ability and contribution to participate in collective actions and collaborative projects, assessable through project outcomes and peer review.

### 3.3 HyperCapital and Well-being Economics

HyperCapital is not intended to create another speculative instrument; its theoretical foundation is "Economics of Well-being." It aims to shift the goal of economic activities from pure profit maximization to enhancing the overall well-being of participant groups. The value of HyperCapital is ultimately manifested through the well-being-enhancing goods, services, or rights it can exchange for. It incentivizes beneficial collaborative behavior for the community rather than zero-sum games.

## 4. Research Design & Methodology

### 4.1 Design Science Research Methodology

Given that this research aims to create an innovative technological-social solution (IT Artifact) to solve existing problems, we adopt **Design Science Research Methodology** (Hevner et al., 2004). This methodology emphasizes acquiring knowledge through constructing and evaluating innovative artifacts. This research follows the six-step process model proposed by Peffers et al. (2007):

1. **Problem Identification and Motivation**: Identifying the extractive problems of platform capitalism (elaborated in the introduction).
2. **Define Solution Objectives**: The goal is to create a decentralized economic model that can achieve fair authentication and value distribution of social capital.
3. **Design and Development**: Designing HyperCapital theory, Mycelium Protocol's technical architecture and economic model.
4. **Demonstration**: Demonstrating how the protocol works through the Zu.Coffee simulation case.
5. **Evaluation**: Evaluating the effectiveness and limitations of this solution through case analysis and comparison with existing theories.
6. **Communication**: Publishing research results in the form of this thesis.

### 4.2 Technical Path: Blockchain as Credit Infrastructure

Choosing blockchain technology as the underlying infrastructure is because it provides the crucial "trustless" consensus and credit foundation for HyperCapital.

### 4.3 Economic Model: Collaborative Innovation and Consensus Economy

The new economic model provides value propositions and distribution principles for HyperCapital, emphasizing value co-creation, consensus pricing, and endogenous circulation.

### 4.4 Case Selection and Data Sources

This research selects the Mycelium Protocol as the concretization of the theoretical framework and Zu.Coffee as the simulation validation case. Case analysis data will come from logical deduction of protocol design, simulated quantitative data tables, and simulated qualitative interview quotes to enhance the credibility and depth of the case.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Interpretation of Findings: Feasibility and Challenges of HyperCapital

This research found through case analysis that HyperCapital theory has logical feasibility at the micro level. However, challenges are equally apparent: scaling difficulties, value anchoring, and governance risks.

### 6.2 Dialogue with Existing Theories

The findings of this research form an interesting dialogue with existing theories. First, HyperCapital can be viewed as a constructive response to Zuboff's (2019) "surveillance capitalism." Zuboff profoundly revealed how "behavioral surplus value" is unilaterally extracted, while HyperCapital provides a technical and economic framework for returning this value to users. Second, it extends Srnicek's (2017) critique of platform capitalism. Srnicek focused on the "extractive" nature of platforms, while this research goes further to explore a "generative" collaborative alternative based on cooperation. Finally, relative to the critical perspective of "digital labor" theory (Fuchs, 2014), HyperCapital provides a potential solution for transforming unpaid labor into quantifiable, compensable assets.

### 6.3 Ethical Risks and Mitigation Strategies

"Everything can be quantified" is an ethical risk that the HyperCapital model must carefully address. It may lead to over-commercialization of social interactions, eroding social relationships based on pure altruism. To mitigate this risk, protocol design can introduce supplementary mechanisms: for example, establishing non-tradable "community contribution badges" that only represent honor; or at the algorithmic level, giving higher reputation weights to non-transactional, purely sharing altruistic behaviors, thereby preserving and encouraging the intrinsic value of social relationships beyond economic incentives.

### 6.4 Practical Implications

This research reveals a possible path beyond existing platform models: from "extracting value" to "co-creating value," from "centralized control" to "decentralized collaboration," from "profit maximization" to "well-being optimization."

## 7. Conclusion & Future Work

### 7.1 Main Conclusions

This research diagnosed the internal contradictions of platform capitalism and proposed an alternative theoretical framework called HyperCapital. Through design science research methods and simulation case analysis, we conclude that the HyperCapital model based on decentralized credit can theoretically incentivize collaboration, achieve fair value distribution, and build well-being-oriented economic micro-cycles, providing a promising path for solving the structural problems of platform capitalism.

### 7.2 Research Limitations

The main limitations of this research lie in its still being in the theoretical exploration and simulation stage, lacking large-scale empirical data support. Additionally, this research's exploration of specific technical implementation details and macroeconomic impacts is not sufficiently deep.

### 7.3 Future Research Prospects

Future research can unfold in the following areas:
1. **Prototype Development and Empirical Research**: Developing a minimum viable product (MVP) of Zu.Coffee for long-term empirical testing.
2. **Interdisciplinary Theoretical Deepening**: Deeper cross-integration of HyperCapital theory with law (digital property rights), political science (decentralized governance), computer science (protocol design), anthropology (gift economy), and other disciplines.
3. **Policy and Regulatory Research**: Exploring public policies and legal frameworks that support the development of such new economic models.
