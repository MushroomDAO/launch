## Chapter 7: Discussion

Our evaluation of the SuperPaymaster system provides strong evidence of its effectiveness in addressing core usability and efficiency challenges in blockchain transactions. This section interprets the significance of these findings, discusses their broader implications for both theory and practice, and candidly addresses the limitations of this study to chart a course for future research.

### 7.1 Interpretation of Findings

The empirical results from our controlled experiment confirm that SuperPaymaster provides a substantially improved user experience over traditional workflows. The system demonstrably reduces the number of interaction steps, the end-to-end transaction time, and the net cost to the user, with all results being statistically significant (p < .001) and showing large effect sizes. This directly answers **RQ1**, providing a clear, validated mechanism for reducing cost and complexity. 

More profoundly, the validation of the "Gas Card" metaphor through expert assessment addresses **RQ2**. The success of this metaphor lies in its ability to bridge what Norman [9] terms the "gulf of execution." It replaces abstract, technically-dense concepts (gas, gwei, nonce) with a single, familiar mental model from the real world. This abstraction is a key driver of the observed reduction in cognitive load, moving beyond mere step reduction to address the fundamental psychological barriers to entry for new users. The strong positive reception from HCI experts confirms that this design choice is not just a convenience but a core contribution to blockchain usability.

Finally, the successful implementation of the prototype, as detailed in Chapter 5 and validated by experts, confirms the feasibility of the competitive architecture proposed to answer **RQ3**. It proves that a system enabling permissionless, competitive gas sponsorship can be built and operated, providing a viable technical alternative to the centralized, oligopolistic solutions currently dominating the market.

### 7.2 Implications of the Study

Our findings carry significant implications for both academic research and industry practice, pushing the boundaries of how user-centric systems are designed in the Web3 space.

**Theoretical Implications:** This research makes two primary contributions to theory. First, it serves as a detailed Design Science Research case study that systematically applies HCI principles, particularly Cognitive Load Theory [40], to address a well-known, persistent problem in the blockchain domain. It provides a concrete example of how to design and evaluate a socio-technical artifact where user perception is as important as technical function. Second, it empirically validates the use of metaphor-driven design as a powerful tool for abstracting complex technical systems. This extends the application of the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) [24, 25] by showing that a focus on Perceived Ease of Use can be achieved through deliberate design interventions, not just underlying technical simplification. Our work argues that for complex systems like blockchain, the design of the *interaction* is a critical and underexplored component of PEOU.

**Practical Implications:** For practitioners, our work offers an open-source framework that enables the creation of more accessible and user-friendly dApps, directly addressing the critical barrier of user onboarding and retention. The competitive, decentralized model for gas sponsorship presents a viable alternative to the centralized services that currently dominate the market, fostering a healthier, more innovative ecosystem free from single points of failure and censorship risk. For dApp developers, this means a simplified integration path and the ability to focus on core application logic. For end-users, this translates to a Web3 experience that is not only cheaper and faster but also fundamentally more intuitive and less intimidating, paving the way for broader mainstream adoption.

### 7.3 Limitations and Future Work

We acknowledge the limitations of this study, which in turn define a clear path for future research. The primary limitation is that our evaluation, while rigorously controlled, was not a large-scale, longitudinal study conducted in a live production environment. Therefore, the long-term economic dynamics and network effects of the competitive market remain to be observed. Secondly, our expert panel, while valuable for design validation, is not a substitute for large-scale usability studies with a diverse, non-expert user base, which would be required to generate a full quantitative measure of usability like a System Usability Scale (SUS) score.

Consequently, we propose three key directions for future work: **(1) Longitudinal Study:** Deploy SuperPaymaster in a production environment for a multi-year study to validate the long-term economic model, observe emergent node operator behaviors, and measure the sustainability of the competitive market. **(2) Large-Scale Usability Testing:** Conduct studies with hundreds of non-expert users to quantitatively measure usability metrics and validate the findings from the expert assessment. **(3) Advanced Security and Economic Modeling:** Future research could use formal methods and game-theoretic modeling to test the architecture's resilience against sophisticated economic attacks, such as collusion, Sybil attacks, and MEV-related vulnerabilities.
