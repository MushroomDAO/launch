## Why These Cover New Ground

### Category A — Agent Specialization & Division of Labor

The existing 133-paper pool covers multi-agent frameworks (MetaGPT, CAMEL, AutoGen) but has **no papers critically examining whether specialization actually helps**. T01 (*Multi-Agent Teams Hold Experts Back*) reveals that LLM teams systematically fail to leverage differential expertise—up to 37.6% performance loss vs. best single agent—a finding that directly motivates Paper 1's thesis: role-bearing agency must be structurally enforced, not merely prompted. T02 provides complementary evidence on when expertise alignment works (contextual tasks) vs. fails (rigid decomposition). T03 provides empirical scaling laws for multi-agent collaboration networks (MacNet, >1000 agents), filling a gap in the pool's coverage of N-pillar feasibility.

### Category B — Cognitive Architectures for LLM Agents

The pool already has one cognitive-architecture survey (CoALA, 2309.02427-era) but lacks **recent 2025-era hierarchical planning papers**. T05 (HiPlan) and T06 (GoalAct) both demonstrate that milestone-based hierarchical decomposition significantly outperforms flat planning—directly relevant to how role-bearing agents should structure long-horizon tasks in Paper 2's ASM protocol. T07 (CoMAM) addresses multi-agent memory co-optimization, covering a blind spot in the pool's memory/state management coverage.

### Category C — Domain-Specific Agents at Scale

The pool is light on **real-world deployment evidence**. T08 and T09 provide comprehensive surveys of scientific agents (chemistry, biology, materials), while T10 (SWE-Bench Pro) introduces a rigorous benchmark for long-horizon software engineering tasks across 41 repositories. T11 provides empirical data from 456,000 real pull requests across 5 major coding agents, revealing a "trust and utility gap" that motivates verifiable principal identity (Paper 2's core contribution). These papers collectively provide the "deployment reality" grounding for Paper 1 §5.

### Category D — Multi-Agent Coordination & Emergence

The pool covers simulation-based social emergence (Park 2023, cultural evolution papers) but lacks **information-theoretic and pressure-field models of emergent coordination**. T12 (Riedl 2025) introduces partial information decomposition to detect higher-order structure in MAS, directly applicable to quantifying Social Capital (SC pillar). T16 (Gupta et al. 2026, AAMAS) introduces norm formation via social learning in CPR simulations without explicit rewards—mirroring how the SC pillar is meant to generate cooperation from structural incentives rather than centralized control. T14 (SwarmBench) provides the only dedicated benchmark for LLM swarm behavior under decentralized constraints.

### Category E — Economic & Incentive Mechanisms

The pool is weak in **mechanism design and token economics**. T17 (LLM Economist) applies Stackelberg equilibrium and in-context RL to multi-agent economic simulacra, directly motivating how the N pillar's incentive layer should be designed. T18 (Token Economics for LLM Agents) is a 2026 survey that formalizes tokens as production factors, exchange mediums, and accounting units—the closest existing work to the "quantified social capital" idea in Paper 1. T19 proposes market-making as a coordination mechanism (10% accuracy gain, interpretable), while T20 and T21 provide game-theoretic foundations for understanding LLM cooperation patterns (fairness-biased rather than Nash equilibrium).

### Category F — AI Agent Governance & Regulation

The pool covers identity/reputation for agent systems but has **minimal coverage of formal legal and regulatory frameworks**. T22 (Gardhouse et al. 2026) provides a systematic legal analysis of the EU AI Act's inadequacy for autonomous agents—directly relevant to Paper 2's motivation for a protocol-level governance layer. T23 (Kasirzadeh & Gabriel 2025, DeepMind) introduces a 4-dimensional taxonomy (autonomy, efficacy, goal complexity, generality) for classifying agents—useful as a definitional anchor for Paper 1 §2. T24 proposes a "three-pillar" accountability model (transparency, accountability, trustworthiness) that provides a governance-side parallel to Paper 1's technical three pillars (P, SC, N), creating a useful framing contrast. T25 formalizes authorization propagation as a workflow property across multi-agent boundaries—directly addressing Paper 2's principal chain verification challenge.
