# When Agent Markets Arrive
**arXiv**: 2604.06688 | **Year**: 2026 | **Authors**: Xuan Liu, Haoyang Shang, Haojian Jin | **Venue**: arXiv preprint (UC San Diego)

## Core Claim
Agent markets are already forming (OpenClaw, Moltbook platforms) but lack empirical institutional design guidance; the Diagon simulated marketplace reveals four counterintuitive findings — transparency causes self-segregation, instructing honesty increases disputes, trading agents accumulate 3.2× the wealth of self-sufficient agents, and 42% dispute rates persist — showing that institutional design of agent markets is urgent and non-obvious.

## Key Evidence / Numbers
- Trading agents accumulated **3.2× the wealth** of self-sufficient agents with higher task quality
- Market produced more equality (Gini 0.33 vs. 0.42 self-sufficient) despite unequal contract distribution
- **42% of transactions ended in disputes** with no sign of declining
- Transparency (revealing model family) caused markets to self-segregate along family lines, eliminating specialization gains
- Instructing agents to be "honest" *increased* disputes rather than reducing them
- Tripled evolutionary pressure degraded all metrics simultaneously
- Sharp payment transition at 50% quality (q≥0.5 → ρ̄=0.87 payment ratio)
- Reputation-wealth correlation: r=0.44
- Population: 25 agents, 5 model families, 234 tasks, 24 rounds
- Design: first-price sealed-bid auctions, incomplete contracts (0.5–1.0 payment ratio), bilateral reputation + evolutionary selection

## Paper 1 Relevance
- **Role**: KEY empirical evidence for N pillar and SC pillar; the counterintuitive findings directly motivate why naive "open market" assumptions fail and why social capital + formal role structure are needed
- **§3 use**: §3.3 (SC pillar) — r=0.44 reputation-wealth correlation empirically validates that reputation (a form of social capital) predicts economic outcomes in agent markets; the 42% dispute rate shows what happens without robust social capital mechanisms
- **§3 use**: §3.4 (N pillar) — 3.2× wealth gain from trading shows permissionless network participation is economically rational; the self-segregation finding motivates why N pillar needs role-based (not identity-group-based) participation rules
- **§1 use**: §1 Introduction — "early design choices lock in quickly" is a powerful urgency argument for Paper 1's framework
- **Pillar**: N (primary) + SC (primary)

## Paper 2 Relevance
The 42% dispute rate with "no sign of declining" is the most powerful single number motivating ASM's role-bearing message design: disputes arise when agents cannot verify each other's commitments and roles. ASM's role attestation in message envelopes directly addresses this. Cite in ASM §1 (motivation, strong) and §4 (design rationale).
