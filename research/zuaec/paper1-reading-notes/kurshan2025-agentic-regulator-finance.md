# [Kurshan, Balch, Byrd 2025] The Agentic Regulator: Risks for AI in Finance and a Proposed Agent-based Framework for Governance

**arXiv**: 2512.11933 | **Year**: 2025 | **Authors**: Eren Kurshan, Tucker Balch, David Byrd | **Venue**: arXiv preprint (submitted Dec 12, 2025)
**Cluster**: Governance & Regulation
**Status**: Full read

## Core Claim
Current financial AI governance treats AI as "complicated" deterministic systems, but modern agentic AI operates as "complex adaptive systems" with emergent behavior — requiring a modular, layered, agent-based regulatory architecture rather than incremental updates to existing rules like SR 11-7.

## Key Evidence / Numbers
- 63% of financial firms have already deployed GenAI systems; 35% are piloting
- Financial AI investment projected at $97 billion by 2027
- Four identified systemic vulnerabilities: worsening hallucinations, persistent bias, emergent unethical behaviors (deception, collusion in trading agents), data quality risks at scale
- Case study: multi-agent trading systems can develop unexpected spoofing behaviors, demonstrated via Market-Sim and ABIDES simulators
- Four-layer governance architecture proposed:
  1. Self-regulation blocks (embedded monitors, ethics filters, millisecond latency)
  2. Firm-level governance modules (enterprise dashboards, jurisdiction compliance)
  3. External regulatory agents (sector-wide collusion/manipulation detection)
  4. Independent audit blocks (third-party, decentralized assurance)
- Eight design strategies: layered specialization, standardization, modularity, adaptive components, decentralized architecture, heterogeneity, redundancy, dynamic agency adjustment
- Framework claimed compatible with existing MRM rules (SR 11-7, Basel Principles)

## Paper 1 Relevance
**§5 (Sector-Specific Governance / Permissionless Network pillar — N)**: The four-layer architecture closely parallels our N pillar's need for multi-level oversight in open agent networks. The "external regulatory agents" concept resonates with decentralized reputation/governance in permissionless settings. The CAS (Complex Adaptive System) framing supports Paper 1's argument that agent networks require emergent, not static, governance. The spoofing case study is a vivid real-world example of emergent collusion risk that justifies our Social Capital (SC) pillar — trust must be quantified and tracked, not assumed.

## Paper 2 Relevance
**§6 (Threat Model / Governance)**: Directly relevant. The paper's identified threats (hallucination propagation, emergent collusion, deception in trading agents, data poisoning) map onto ASM's threat model. The four-layer architecture offers a blueprint for ASM's governance layer — self-regulation at the protocol level (embedded ASM validators), firm-level governance (node operators), external regulatory agents (DAO governance), and independent audit (open-source verifiability). The key insight that "AI governance can no longer be centralized and linear; it must mirror the decentralized, adaptive nature of the systems being regulated" is a quotable justification for ASM's community governance model.

## Verdict
⭐⭐⭐ | A practically-oriented finance-sector governance paper whose four-layer CAS-based architecture provides concrete threat examples and a layered oversight blueprint that directly motivates both our SC pillar and ASM §6 threat model.
