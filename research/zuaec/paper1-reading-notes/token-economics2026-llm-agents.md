## Core Claim

Tokens are not merely computational units but the core economic primitives of agentic AI — functioning simultaneously as production factors, exchange mediums, and units of account — requiring a unified four-level economic framework (micro/meso/macro/security) to address the exponential consumption bottlenecks they create.

## Key Evidence / Numbers

Note: full quantitative results not available from abstract/redirect; the following are structural claims from the paper:
- **Four analytical dimensions**: micro (single agent), meso (multi-agent), macro (ecosystem), security (adversarial)
- Tokens cause **exponential consumption bottlenecks** in agentic pipelines
- Frontier directions: "differentiable token budgets and dynamic markets" for scalable agent systems
- Claimed to be the **first comprehensive survey** integrating CS and economics for token-level agent economics

## Paper 1 Relevance

Directly supports **§4 (SC pillar — Quantified Social Capital)**. The four-level taxonomy maps cleanly onto our three-pillar architecture: micro = individual agent capability accounting (P pillar), meso = inter-agent social capital transactions (SC pillar), macro = network-wide incentive design (N pillar). The "tokens as economic primitives" framing provides a concrete instantiation of what "quantified social capital" means at the implementation level.

Key citation angle: this paper establishes the theoretical vocabulary for treating agent interactions as economic transactions — essential foundation for arguing that SC must be a first-class architectural pillar rather than an afterthought.

## Paper 2 Relevance

Highest relevance for **§5 (N pillar — economic incentives)**. The meso-level transaction cost framing directly justifies ASM's reputation-weighted routing: by reducing collaboration friction between agents with established reputations, the protocol lowers the "transaction costs" of multi-agent work. The macro-level congestion pricing discussion supports our argument for network-level resource allocation mechanisms in ASM.

SC pillar: principal-agent theory at meso level is exactly the theoretical basis for ASM's role-based trust model — agents operate as principals delegating to specialised sub-agents, with social capital serving as the bonding mechanism.
